Customer Guides

Everything you need to know

From choosing the right tile to keeping it looking perfect for decades. Our architect-curated guides help you make the best decisions for your space.

Care & Cleaning

Keeping your ceramics perfect

🧹
Daily Cleaning
Sweep or vacuum loose dirt and debris daily. Use a damp mop with warm water for regular cleaning. Avoid excess water pooling on grout lines. For floor tiles in high-traffic areas, clean at least twice a week to maintain shine and prevent buildup.
🧴
Cleaning Products
Use pH-neutral ceramic tile cleaners. Avoid acidic cleaners like vinegar or lemon juice — they damage grout and dull glazed surfaces over time. For stubborn stains, a mild detergent with warm water works well. Never use abrasive scrubbers on glazed or polished tiles.
✨
Keeping the Shine
After mopping, dry the floor with a clean cloth or mop to prevent water spots. For polished tiles, use a specialist tile polish every few months. Matte tiles require no polishing — simply clean and dry. Avoid wax-based products as they leave a residue.
🔧
Grout Maintenance
Grout is porous and absorbs stains easily. Seal grout lines every 12–18 months with a grout sealer. For cleaning, use a grout brush and a baking soda paste. Avoid bleach on colored grout. Re-grout any cracked or crumbling areas promptly to prevent water damage.
🚫
What to Avoid
Never use steel wool, wire brushes, or harsh abrasive pads. Avoid bleach on natural stone tiles. Do not use ammonia-based cleaners on marble or travertine surfaces. Keep heavy furniture from dragging across floor tiles — use felt pads under legs to prevent scratching.
💧
Bathroom & Wet Areas
In bathrooms and wet rooms, squeegee walls after each use to prevent soap scum and mold buildup. Apply a tile-and-grout sealer to wall tiles in showers. Ensure adequate ventilation to reduce moisture. Clean with a bathroom-specific tile cleaner weekly.
Installation Guide

Installing your tiles correctly

01

Prepare the Surface

The surface must be clean, dry, flat and structurally sound. Remove any existing flooring, adhesive residue, paint or dust. Check for levelness — use a long spirit level and fill any dips with floor leveling compound. Allow to cure fully before tiling. A poorly prepared surface is the most common cause of tile failure.

02

Plan Your Layout

Dry-lay tiles across the room first without adhesive to plan the pattern. Start from the center of the room and work outward so cuts are equal at both walls. Mark reference lines with chalk. For patterns or large-format tiles like 60×120cm, planning is critical — even a small misalignment is very visible at scale.

03

Choose the Right Adhesive

Use a flexible tile adhesive rated for your tile size and surface type. For large-format tiles (60×60 and above), use a C2 grade flexible adhesive. Apply with a notched trowel — both to the floor and the back of large tiles (back-buttering). Coverage must be at least 85% for floor tiles, 95% for wet areas.

04

Lay the Tiles

Press each tile firmly into the adhesive with a slight twisting motion. Use tile spacers for consistent grout joints — 2mm for polished tiles, 3–5mm for textured. Check each tile is level with its neighbors using a rubber mallet and spirit level. Work in small sections so adhesive does not skin over before you lay the tile.

05

Allow to Cure

Leave tiles undisturbed for a minimum of 24 hours — 48 hours for large-format tiles. Do not walk on the tiled area during this time. Curing time increases in cold or humid conditions. Check the adhesive manufacturer's instructions for the specific product you are using.

06

Grout and Finish

Remove tile spacers before grouting. Apply grout with a rubber float, pressing firmly into joints at a 45° angle. Remove excess grout from tile surfaces with a damp sponge before it dries — work in circular motions. Allow to dry for 24–48 hours then buff tiles with a dry cloth. Seal grout lines within one week of installation.

How to Choose

Choosing the right tile for your space

By Location
Floor vs Wall
Floor tiles must be rated for slip resistance (R9 or higher for wet areas). Wall tiles can be thinner and more decorative. Never use wall tiles on floors — they are not designed for foot traffic and will crack. Floor tiles can be used on walls, though they are heavier.
By Finish
Matte vs Glossy
Matte tiles hide dirt and scratches better — ideal for floors and high-traffic areas. Glossy tiles reflect light and make spaces feel larger — ideal for walls, bathrooms and feature areas. Polished tiles are the most elegant but show every footprint and require more maintenance.
By Size
Small vs Large Format
Large-format tiles (60×60, 60×120) create a seamless, luxurious look with fewer grout lines. They work best in spacious rooms. Small tiles and mosaics work well in compact spaces, curves and feature walls. The tile size should be proportional to the room — oversized tiles in a small bathroom look awkward.
By Room
Kitchen Specifics
Kitchen floors need slip resistance and stain resistance — matte or textured tiles are ideal. Kitchen backsplashes can use glossy tiles as they are easy to wipe clean. Avoid light-colored grout in cooking areas as it stains easily. Darker grout hides oil and food splashes much better.
By Room
Bathroom Specifics
Wet areas need slip-resistant floor tiles (R10 minimum). Large-format tiles reduce grout lines, which means less mold risk. Lighter colors make small bathrooms feel larger. Consider heated floor compatibility if you plan underfloor heating — most ceramic tiles conduct heat well.
By Budget
Value vs Premium
Buy 10–15% more tiles than your calculated area to account for cuts and future repairs. Never mix batches if possible — color can vary between production batches. Investing in good quality adhesive and grout is as important as the tile itself. A premium tile poorly installed will always disappoint.
